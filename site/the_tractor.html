The Tractor

by Roger Ellingson

As you know, the Ainsworth Star Journal prints all the news that's worthy of print. We were the ones who told you about how John and Mary Larson had visitors from Omaha last month. Mary made her apple crumb cake and it was delicious. Well, we got a call from somebody who works at Bejot Feed Lots north of town telling us how there was this tractor we just had to see to believe. He said it had triple tires front and back, looked like it could hold more attachments than a Swiss Army knife, and had more dials and switches than some airliners. We thought it was interesting so we sent a new reporter, who majored in journalism down in Kearney, out to interview the farmer and this is what we were able to gather from his notes.

The farmer met the kid in the yard and said, "You must be the guy who wants to see my tractor and write about it. The kid said, "Well, yeah, but how did you know that? The farmer answered, "I spotted your car 'bout a mile and a half out and it wasn't a Chevy or Ford so I figured it had to be you. Now, if you wanna see the tractor let's get going 'cause there's four hours of daylight left and I got things to do."

Around the corner of the barn, the kid stood with mouth open in astonishment from his first look at the tractor. The farmer let him take it in for as long as he liked. Then, the kid shook his head and said, "This doesn't look like any tractor I ever saw. It's got triple tires, front and back, bends in the middle like a payloader, looks like it could hold more attachments than a Swiss Army knife and..." The farmer interrupted him with a suspicious look and said, "Sounds like you been talking to that guy who works for Bejot's." The kid look puzzled for a second or two so the farmer said, "Never mind. What would you like to know about the tractor?"

"Er, just what kind of tractor is this?" asked the kid, and the farmer answered, "It's a John Deere 9400 articulated farm tractor."

"What does the 9400 stand for?"

"I'm not sure I remember but I think it's either its horsepower in thousands or its towing capacity in megatons."

"Geez, that's a lot of power. What on Earth would take that much power?"

"Well, I like it for irrigation"

The kid looked confused.

"Yup, if the electric company wants to shut off your pivots during the day, you just drag the whole farm up to Meadville, soak it in the Niobrara for a couple of hours, and drag it back. You can fill the keg while you're there too."

"The keg?"

"Yeah. See that saddle tank on the left nose of the tractor? That's for beer. Gets mighty hot up here in the sandhills."

"Don't you have air conditioning in the tractor?"

"Of course I do. Keeps the beer cold. You aint from around here are you, son? But while we're about it, you see that other saddle tank on the right? That's for the still."

"The still?"

"Yeah, it's for the tractor. If you're harvesting corn, you can make ethanol and if you're harvesting soybeans, you can make biodiesel. Saves about half a percent on fuel."

"Well, I suppose every little bit helps but what does something like that cost?"

"Twenty-two thousand dollars."

"Twenty-two thousand dollars? Twenty-two thousand dollars? Twenty-two thousand dollars?"

"Son, all the fuel we use, it'll pay for itself in two seasons. Ya gotta understand how a farmer's got to invest a hundred thousand dollars to make ten."

"Well, that doesn't sound too bad ... 10 percent payback?"

"Son, you're not listening. A farmer has to put out a hundred thousand dollars to make ten dollars. We couldn't even make it if my wife didn't work at the Coop. A farmer may raise his own beef but sometimes it's the wife who brings home the bacon. Now, would you like to see the cab?"

"Uh, sure." So the climbed into the cab and the kid said, "Wow, this is bigger than my bathroom."

"Oh, it's got a bathroom. The DEQ wants you to fill out paperwork if you take a pee in your cornfield. They call it nitrogen management. I don't mind the extra expense though 'cause it saves me exposing myself to the elements in the winter. Now let me show you the butt switch."

"The butt switch?"

"Yeah," farmer lifting up the seat, "you see those two wires there? If you're not sitting down when the PTO is on, it shuts down everything."

"That sounds like a good safety feature."

"Well, maybe. But that ain't what it's for. It's so if you spot a deer or a coyote, you can grab your rifle and not have to worry about the the tractor messing up your aim.

“Now, look at this here speed control. They call it an infinitely variable transmission. One day, I headed out to one field and that was 320 acres, about a mile long, so I just thought I'd test it out and I ran it all the way to that figure-8 laying on it side. The next moment, the whole world flashed, light-n-dark, so I pulled it back an found myself done with the field, and watched somebody in my tractor — somebody who looked like me — just coming out of the yard to the same field. I asked the science teacher at the school about it and he said it involved faster-than-light speed and backwards time travel. You'd have to be some kind of Einstein to understand it. My wife had a few questions, too, but I won't go into that."

"I see," said the kid.

"Oh, I forgot to tell you about the zero-altitude ejection seat."

"Beg your pardon?"

"Yeah, with these new pivots, we're farming some hills and gulleys that would make a billy goat pucker. If the tractor starts to tip over, this thing will blow the top off the cab, then take you sideways and up 500 feet and then land you safely with a parachute."

"Wow! That's amazing but wouldn't a roll bar offer enough protection?"

"Well, maybe. But John Deere did a study and they found out that an ejection seat would be more effective in generating revenue."

"OK, but let me step back a minute. Isn't Nebraska sort of flat?"

"Now I know you're not from around here. Look around you here. The only flat land you'll find is where Land Levelers came in and filled a valley with a hill for gravity irrigation. The only natural flat land in Nebraska is down in the Platte Valley. Even the name, Nebraska, comes from a Niobrara Indian word that means, "Not flat here."

"Ok, I understand," though he didn't, "but now I'm curious about what appears to be several kinds of air filtration. Can you tell me more about them?"

"Oh yeah, this here is a heap filter, this its an Aaronizer, and this is a Quadra from Sharper image. Doesn't let anything in the cab you don't want."

"Wow again! That'd be great for people with allergies."

"Maybe, but mostly it's to keep out all the chemicals we use in farming these days. Haven't you ever wondered why farmers don't have big families anymore?"

"I see, but what's this green bottle."

"Oh, that's oxygen. John Deere throws that in under their 50/50 plan. If you've been farming over 50 years or your waistline is over 50 inches, they figure it's cheaper than trying to collect from estate sales."

"OooKaayyy. Now, what is this display panel for? Is and equipment monitor or what?"

"Naw, that's a DVD screen so I can watch movies while I'm farming."

"How can you watch movies while you're operating the tractor?"

"That's what GPS is for, son. They call it Auto Trac or something like that. Didn't you notice I ain't got a normal steering wheel? They took our the original and put in this weird-looking dome thing with all new hydraulics and there's some transmitter out at John Deere they call RTK that helps steer the tractor, hands-off, to within an inch of where you want it. The first year I tried it, on trial mind you, I got 30 more rows out of one fields 'cause it made the guess-rows narrower than I ever dared before. That's when I got the DVD player. The GPS pauses it at the end of the field so you can make sure you don't take out fences or trees, and lets you push the play button once you're turned around for the next 24 rows."

"What kind of movies do you watch?"

"Well, my wife don't like me watching them but she don't read the paper so I guess I can tell you. "I've got all of the Rambo movies and every episode of Walker, Texas Ranger."

By this time, the kid was suffering information overload, and he quit us, going back to college as an English major but he did have the intestinal fortitude to asks one more question. "Is there anything else you would like our readers to know?"

"Just this. If that guy from Bejot's comes snooping around my equipment again, I'm gonna find out if that butt switch works the way I told you."
