Cowboy Up

from "The Book of Residue"
by Roger Ellingson

It was the "National Day of the Cowboy" for three days here in Ainsworth -- from Friday, July 20 thru Sunday, July 22, 2007. We had more activities going on here to celebrate 'the cowboy' than we did for the Fourth of July. Most of the events were at the Daniels' Manufacturing show grounds east of town where, this year, they built an impressive entry arch out of multiple telephone poles, cut down in stair-step fashion, and all locals were invited to bring their branding irons so, as they made and entrance, they could also help 'make an entrance.' We also had parades thru town with horses and mules pulling wagons with wooden wheels and steel rims. (For city folk, a mule is a sterile cross between a horse and a donkey -- and, while steel-rimmed wheels make a lot of racket on paved roads, they made a lot more sense than letting the wood wear out and having the wheel break apart on the prairie.)

Some of the rides and floats in the parade carried saloon girls and most of them looked so good in their billowing red dresses that it explains part of why going to a saloon was so popular in days past. Saloon girls were, to America, what the Geisha was/is to Japan. By definition, they were not 'escorts' -- "The Unsinkable Molly Brown" was a saloon girl for a while -- and they were often crusaders in the rights of women, mine workers, and children.

But let's get back to the celebration. We had dozen's of people on horses with guns and, along with semi-rodeo activities, we had a stage-coach-and-bank-robbery. Last year, I got there just in time to see a poor soul fall off his horse and I thought it was just a robber who'd been shot by the guy riding 'shot-gun' on the stage coach. It turns out that he really did fall and got injured. It was fun; it was drama and, as indicated, some of it was very real. The question remains: How much of the cowboy legend is real and how much is mere romance like the so-called 'good old days?'

Legend has it that the Spanish taught the rest of the world how to handle cattle. Indeed, the word 'caballero' (kah-bah-YAY-row) invokes the image of a cowboy in Argentina. with boleo in hand, for snaring the legs of 'bacas' (cattle) that need to be branded. The image is wrong, however, because a Caballero is a Spanish gentleman or cavalier. It comes from the latin 'caballārius', horse groom, and, while it does mean a man who is skilled in riding and managing horses, the gentlemanly aspect rules out cowboys as we know them. Rather, a caballero is one who keeps horses for breeding or racing or as a hobby of the rich. (I'm not saying that cowboys are not gentlemen; I'm saying that cowboys, as I know them, are not of the gentry -- they are not what you would call genteel and they would be insulted if you called them that.)

The Spanish word for an American cowboy is vaquero (bah-KAY-row), and the English corruption of this word is buckeroo. An hispanic cowboy is called a caporal (KAH-poe-RAHL), a vague contraction of capo (captain) and corral, which translates to mean 'ruler of the corral'. The distinction is made because the machismo of the latin culture insists that non-hispanics (gringos) are mere boys by comparison. Another spanish word for cowboy is cuatrero (kwah-TRAY-row), meaning bandido, ladrón, delincuente—in english it means rustler—one who is despised for morals but grudgingly admired for his audacity. In this part of the country, such audacity is not so admired; just north of Ainsworth is Meadville, the heart of vigilante hangings and shootings between 1880 and the early 1900's that dealt with the horse-and-cattle thieving of 10-20 people, including the notorious 'Doc' Middleton who kept his stolen livery in a hidden corral just 6 miles west of Meadville. Keya Paha county and is still referred to as Mob County.

This still doesn't address the question of 'What is a Cowboy?' and 'Why do we bother celebrating the day of the cowboy?" TV shows in the 50's-60's about cowboys abounded and my father always took charge of the TV when they came on in the days before remotes. I thought it was just because of his own machismo (I think he thought of himself as Chuck Conners in 'The Rifleman') but, since I moved to this area, I have come to appreciate how the cowboy is as real these days as he was in the days of driving cattle some hundreds of miles a century or more ago to the nearest railroad line.

These days, cowboys work on ranches and they ride horses, ATVs, or pickups. They go out 24/7/365, in summer drought or winter blizzard, to check the cattle, put out hay or minerals, wash waters, help cows in calving, doctor the sick and bury the dead. Sometimes they drive the cattle to new pastures as needed to keep them fed and healthy. They rope cattle and wrestle them to the ground for branding, tagging -- or doctoring in the field. Sometimes they have the luxury of driving the herd to holding pens where they can be processed more easily in alleys, snakes and chutes but more often it's a temporary corral made of panels carried to the pasture on a special trailer. If just one critter needs to be pulled for doctoring whatever reason, I've seen a film of a single cowboy on horseback walking a cow this way and that until it decided on its own that it wanted to climb into a horse trailer parked out in the middle of a pasture.

Cowboys also break and train horses, something most of us wouldn't think of doing because most people who do so get injured seriously and often. (If a horse is cowboy-broke and you're not a cowboy, don't ride it and don't buy it unless you have a real cowboy to ride it.) But cowboys can't get enough of this stuff. Most of them I know have at least once engaged in rodeos or wild horse races as a test of their own mettle. Some would say it is more because their egos are as great as all outdoors, and they need to show off (www.sharkonline.org) or to attract the opposite sex— women do seem attracted to cowboys like rock stars.

Many cowboys I know have harsh backgrounds. There is a 'Horatio Alger' aspect to their efforts—rodeo purses can be tens of thousands of dollars—but the real cowboy does what he does as a matter of personal pride in doing a job to the best of his ability without regard to his own risk of life and limb. I'll never have the right stuff to be a cowboy but I have punched a charging critter or pushed its head down and aside just to save its life and mine; a sick animal needs to be pulled and doctored and, sometimes, you have to play chicken with it to get it out of the pen into the hospital. (I've been injured enough to need doctoring myself but never as often or as badly as the cowboys I know.)

In a wild horse race, you (and two helpers) have to put a bridle and saddle on an unknown horse out in the open, race it to a goal and back, then take off the saddle and bridle in a race against others who may be coming from any direction while you are tending your own beast. Some cowboys complain about how they made it illegal to bite the horse's ears.

All of the skills that cowboys develop make sense to me for looking after cattle except for one rodeo event which involves cheesing-off 2000 pounds of hamburger by jumping on its back. Cowboys seem to live for the intensity, like race car drivers, base-jumpers and decathletes and they generally drink and fight more than the rest of us. One cowboy I talked to said something like, "It makes me feel more alive when I come close to death." The closest I ever came to that was when one critter charged me in the corner of an alley. I held myself up by my elbows on the top rails of the fence corner and pulled my knees to my chest as it bucked my shins, repeatedly, trying to gore me with its lack-of-horns. I was just glad we cut the horns on this critter a few months earlier.

Cowboys sometimes work as pen-riders in feedlots but most pen-riders are not cowboys. Most are simply people who have learned enough thru training or osmosis to be able to spot a sick animal and who have the mobility to drive that animal or run from it as needed. We still call them cowboys but there are some like me who do not ride horses and there are some who ride horses who will never be cowboys. There is a constant battle between cowboys and farmers just like in the old west but, today, the battle is more in the form of practical jokes or good insults. Good insults are hard to come by so you should appreciate them.

I guess I'm more of a farmer because I was in the hospital pushing cattle one day when Cowboy Blake was calling out about five kinds of meds to be used on one critter already in the chute. I said, "Hey, Blake! With all these things you need to know about treating cattle, I don't know why they say cowboys are dumb."

He looked at me, knowing I was pulling his chain, and said, "Well, Roger, you know cowboys really are dumb. If a critter is standing outside when it's 20-below, it has no choice, but a cowboy goes out there because he wants to be there."
